\begin{abstract}

Deploying reinforcement learning policies in safety-critical robotic systems demands provable constraint satisfaction. Existing safe RL methods either use soft penalty-based constraints sensitive to hyperparameter tuning, or require real-time obstacle detection that is difficult to guarantee in practice. We propose a unified framework with three components: (1)~a manifold safety filter that projects policy actions onto the constraint manifold tangent space via null-space projection, with a reward calibration mechanism that guides the policy to learn inherently safe behaviors; (2)~offline Hamilton-Jacobi reachability analysis that pre-computes environment-aware safe regions from trajectory data, adaptively modulating safety margins at deployment without real-time sensing; and (3)~a data-driven EKF with CNN-learned noise parameters that adapts measurement covariance to velocity-dependent sensor noise. We prove that the manifold projection guarantees constraint satisfaction under mild regularity conditions. Both the reachability module and noise adapter are computed offline and deployed zero-shot. Experiments on Safety-Gymnasium show near-zero constraint violations across Goal, Circle, and Push tasks under varying penalty settings, while baselines exhibit up to $6\times$ cost fluctuation. Zero-shot deployment on a Webots E-puck robot with a simple P-controller achieves zero collisions across 20 trials, demonstrating policy-agnostic transferability.

\end{abstract}

\section{INTRODUCTION}

Ensuring safety is essential for deploying robots in real-world environments such as autonomous driving and service robotics, yet remains difficult due to environmental complexity, dynamic obstacles, and sensor noise. Reinforcement Learning (RL) offers a promising path toward adaptive control through environmental interaction~\cite{gu2024saferl_survey}, but its trial-and-error nature poses safety risks during both training and deployment.

Safe Reinforcement Learning (SafeRL) formulates this problem as Constrained Markov Decision Processes (CMDPs)~\cite{altman1999cmdp}. Existing approaches can be categorized into two main classes:

\textbf{Cost-based methods} balance reward and cost through adaptive penalties. PID Lagrangian~\cite{stooke2020responsive}, interior-point~\cite{liu2022ipo}, and soft barrier~\cite{wang2023soft_barrier} methods improve constraint satisfaction but employ \emph{soft constraints} that often fail to achieve zero-cost performance~\cite{liu2024safe_survey}.

\textbf{State-constrained methods} enforce safety via CBFs~\cite{dawson2023safe_cbf, as2022cbfrl}, recovery policies~\cite{thananjeyan2021recovery}, or constraint manifold projection~\cite{liu2022atacom, liu2023safe_manipulation}. However, they typically require \emph{online} environmental sensing and accurate state estimation, both difficult to guarantee in practice.

\textbf{Contributions.} This paper proposes a unified framework for state-constrained safe RL that replaces real-time obstacle detection with offline reachability pre-computation, with three main contributions:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Constraint Manifold Filter with Reward Calibration}: We develop a null-space projection method that transforms any policy action into a provably safe action by projecting onto the tangent space of the constraint manifold (Section~\ref{sec:manifold}). Unlike prior work~\cite{liu2022atacom}, our method incorporates dynamic reward calibration that penalizes correction magnitude, guiding the policy toward safe behaviors without external penalty tuning.

    \item \textbf{Offline Safe Region Construction}: We introduce \emph{offline} Hamilton-Jacobi reachability analysis to pre-compute environment-aware safe regions from collected trajectory data (Section~\ref{sec:reachability}). Given a prior map of static obstacles (e.g., from offline mapping or simulation), the pre-computed feasibility value function adaptively modulates safety margins at deployment, removing the need for real-time obstacle detection or tracking.

    \item \textbf{Data-Driven State Estimation}: We propose a data-driven EKF with CNN-learned noise parameters that adapts measurement covariance to motion dynamics, maintaining safety filter accuracy under velocity-dependent sensor noise (Section~\ref{sec:ekf}).
\end{enumerate}

The reachability analysis and state estimation modules share a \emph{dual offline precomputation} design: HJ reachability learns environment-aware safety margins from trajectory data, while the CNN-based noise adapter learns velocity-dependent sensor characteristics from exploration data---both are computed offline and deployed zero-shot, requiring no online model updates.

We validate on Safety-Gymnasium~\cite{ji2023safety_gymnasium} and demonstrate that the safety layer is \emph{policy-agnostic} by deploying it on a Webots E-puck robot with a simple P-controller, achieving zero collisions without retraining.

\section{RELATED WORK}
\label{sec:related}

\textbf{Safe RL.} Recent surveys~\cite{gu2024saferl_survey, liu2024safe_survey} categorize SafeRL into cost-based and state-constrained approaches. Cost-based methods optimize a penalty-augmented objective: PID Lagrangian~\cite{stooke2020responsive} adapts multipliers via a PID controller, IPO~\cite{liu2022ipo} uses log-barrier penalties, and soft barrier methods~\cite{wang2023soft_barrier} maintain feasibility probabilistically. While these approaches are general-purpose, they provide \emph{soft} constraint satisfaction---the violation rate depends critically on penalty hyperparameters, and zero-cost performance is rarely achieved~\cite{liu2024safe_survey}.

State-constrained methods enforce safety through action-space restrictions. Control Barrier Functions (CBFs)~\cite{dawson2023safe_cbf, as2022cbfrl} modify actions to keep the system within a forward-invariant safe set, but require a valid CBF---often difficult to construct for complex environments. Recovery RL~\cite{thananjeyan2021recovery} learns recovery zones offline and switches to a learned recovery policy when the agent approaches unsafe regions, but its safety depends on the quality of the learned recovery boundary.

ATACOM~\cite{liu2022atacom, liu2023safe_manipulation} takes a different approach by projecting actions onto the tangent space of the constraint manifold via null-space projection, providing provable constraint satisfaction for robotic manipulation tasks. However, ATACOM assumes \emph{accurate} state information and \emph{known} obstacle positions at each timestep---requirements that are difficult to guarantee in practice with noisy sensors and partially observable environments. Our work extends this manifold projection paradigm in three ways: (i) offline reachability analysis pre-computes environment-aware safety margins from a prior map, replacing the need for real-time obstacle detection and tracking, (ii) data-driven EKF provides robust state estimation under sensor noise, and (iii) reward calibration guides the policy toward safe behaviors without external penalty tuning.

\textbf{HJ Reachability.} Hamilton-Jacobi (HJ) reachability analysis~\cite{bansal2017hj_reachability} computes backward reachable tubes (BRTs) by solving the HJ variational inequality (HJ-VI), providing formal safety guarantees via controlled-invariant sets. Recent work addresses scalability: neural network approximations~\cite{hsu2021safety, so2023rtreach} enable reachability computation in higher dimensions. However, most methods require online computation or access to the environment dynamics model. We instead adopt an offline, data-driven approach: we discretize the HJ-VI into a feasibility Bellman operator and solve it from collected trajectory data using expectile regression~\cite{kostrikov2022iql}, approximating the BRT value function without explicit dynamics. Given a prior map of static obstacles, this decouples safety margin computation from deployment---beneficial for resource-constrained robotic platforms.

\textbf{Learned State Estimation.} Classical EKF assumes known, fixed noise statistics, which may not hold under varying operating conditions. Learning-based methods address this limitation: deep IMU bias inference~\cite{buchanan2022learning_ekf} learns IMU biases for visual-inertial odometry, while TLIO~\cite{liu2023denoise_imu} uses CNNs for tight inertial odometry. KalmanNet~\cite{revach2022kalmannet} learns Kalman gains end-to-end, and A-KIT~\cite{levy2024akit} uses transformers for adaptive process noise estimation. Our CNN-based noise adapter is lightweight (5K parameters) and specifically designed to learn velocity-dependent measurement covariance from IMU windows, providing an adaptive $R_t$ that improves state estimation accuracy for the downstream safety filter.

\section{PROBLEM FORMULATION}
\label{sec:problem}

\subsection{Safe Robot Navigation}

Consider a robot navigating in an environment with $n$ obstacles. Let $s_t = [p_t^\top, v_t^\top]^\top \in \mathcal{S} \subseteq \mathbb{R}^d$ denote the robot state at discrete time $t$, where $p_t \in \mathbb{R}^{d_p}$ is the position and $v_t \in \mathbb{R}^{d_p}$ is the velocity. We define safety through distance-based constraints:
\begin{equation}
    c_i(s_t) := d_{\text{safe}} - \|p_t - o_i\|_2 \leq 0, \quad \forall i \in \{1, \ldots, n\}
    \label{eq:constraint}
\end{equation}
where $d_{\text{safe}} > 0$ is the minimum safety distance, $o_i \in \mathbb{R}^{d_p}$ denotes the position of obstacle $i$, and $\|\cdot\|_2$ denotes the Euclidean norm. A state $s_t$ is \emph{safe} if $c_i(s_t) \leq 0$ for all $i$, meaning the robot maintains at least distance $d_{\text{safe}}$ from all obstacles.

\subsection{Constrained Markov Decision Process}

We formalize safe RL as a CMDP $(\mathcal{S}, \mathcal{A}, P, R, \gamma, C)$ with state space $\mathcal{S}$, action space $\mathcal{A}$, transition kernel $P$, reward $R$, discount $\gamma$, and constraint set $C = \{c_i: \mathcal{S} \rightarrow \mathbb{R}\}_{i=1}^k$. With $k = n$ constraints (one per obstacle), the objective is to find $\pi: \mathcal{S} \rightarrow \mathcal{A}$ maximizing expected return under \emph{hard constraints}:
\begin{equation}
    \max_{\pi} \mathbb{E}_{\pi}\left[ \sum_{t=0}^{T} \gamma^t R(s_t, a_t) \right] \quad \text{s.t.} \quad c_i(s_t) \leq 0, \; \forall t, i
    \label{eq:cmdp}
\end{equation}

\subsection{Control-Affine System Dynamics}

Due to the prevalence of nonholonomic constraints in robotic systems, we model the dynamics as a control-affine system:
\begin{equation}
    \dot{s} = f(s) + G(s) a
    \label{eq:dynamics}
\end{equation}
where $a \in \mathcal{A} \subseteq \mathbb{R}^m$ is the $m$-dimensional control action, $f: \mathcal{S} \rightarrow \mathbb{R}^d$ is the drift term representing uncontrolled dynamics, and $G: \mathcal{S} \rightarrow \mathbb{R}^{d \times m}$ is the control input matrix mapping actions to state derivatives. For a point robot with single-integrator dynamics, $f(s) = 0$ and $G(s) = I$; for a differential-drive robot, $G(s)$ encodes the kinematic constraints.

\section{METHOD}

\subsection{Constraint Manifold Filter}
\label{sec:manifold}

We define the \emph{constraint manifold} as the set of configurations satisfying all safety constraints.

\subsubsection{Slack Variable Formulation}

To handle inequality constraints $c(s) \leq 0$, we introduce non-negative slack variables $\mu \in \mathbb{R}^k_{\geq 0}$ that transform inequalities into equalities:
\begin{equation}
    \bar{c}(s, \mu) := c(s) + \mu = 0, \quad \mu \geq 0
    \label{eq:slack}
\end{equation}
Since $\mu \geq 0$, any state on the constraint manifold satisfies $c(s) = -\mu \leq 0$, directly enforcing the safety constraint. The constraint manifold is then:
\begin{equation}
    \mathcal{M} := \{ (s, \mu) \in \mathcal{D} \mid \bar{c}(s, \mu) = 0 \}
\end{equation}
where $\mathcal{D} = \mathcal{S} \times \mathbb{R}^k_{\geq 0}$ is the augmented state space. Note that $J_\mu = \frac{\partial \bar{c}}{\partial \mu} = I_k$, which is always full rank, ensuring well-conditioned null-space projection.

\subsubsection{Null-Space Projection}

Let $a_{\text{unsafe}} \in \mathbb{R}^m$ denote the unconstrained action output by the RL policy. The safety filter internally constructs the augmented reference vector $u_{\text{ref}} = [a_{\text{unsafe}}^\top, \mathbf{0}^\top]^\top \in \mathbb{R}^{m+k}$, where $k$ is the number of constraints (slack variables), and transforms it into a safe action $[a_{\text{safe}}^\top, \dot{\mu}^\top]^\top$ that satisfies all constraints.

Taking the time derivative of the constraint:
\begin{equation}
    \frac{d}{dt} \bar{c}(s, \mu) = J_s \dot{s} + J_\mu \dot{\mu} = J_s f(s) + J_c \begin{bmatrix} a \\ \dot{\mu} \end{bmatrix}
    \label{eq:constraint_deriv}
\end{equation}
where $J_s = \frac{\partial \bar{c}}{\partial s}$, $J_\mu = \frac{\partial \bar{c}}{\partial \mu}$, and $J_c = [J_s G(s), J_\mu] \in \mathbb{R}^{k \times (m+k)}$ is the combined constraint Jacobian.

To maintain the system on the constraint manifold, we require $\frac{d}{dt}\bar{c} = 0$. Let $J_c^+$ denote the Moore--Penrose pseudoinverse of $J_c$, and define the null-space projector:
\begin{equation}
    N_c = I - J_c^+ J_c
\end{equation}

The safe action is then computed as:
\begin{equation}
    \begin{bmatrix} a_{\text{safe}} \\ \dot{\mu} \end{bmatrix} = N_c \, u_{\text{ref}} - J_c^+ \bigl( J_s f(s) + K_c \bar{c}(s, \mu) \bigr)
    \label{eq:safe_action}
\end{equation}
where $K_c > 0$ is a correction gain. The first term projects the augmented reference onto the constraint null-space (tangent space of $\mathcal{M}$), and the second simultaneously compensates for drift dynamics $f(s)$ and drives the state toward the manifold.

\begin{theorem}[Safety Guarantee]
\label{thm:safety}
Assume that $J_c$ has full row rank along the system trajectory. If the system is initialized on the constraint manifold, i.e., $\bar{c}(s_0, \mu_0) = 0$ with $\mu_0 \geq 0$, and the action is computed via~\eqref{eq:safe_action}, then $c(s_t) \leq 0$ for all $t \geq 0$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Since $J_c$ has full row rank, $J_c J_c^+ = I_k$ and $J_c N_c = 0$. Substituting~\eqref{eq:safe_action} into~\eqref{eq:constraint_deriv}:
\begin{align*}
\dot{\bar{c}} &= J_s f(s) + J_c \bigl[ N_c \, u_{\text{ref}} - J_c^+ (J_s f(s) + K_c \bar{c}) \bigr] \\
&= J_s f(s) - J_c J_c^+ J_s f(s) - K_c J_c J_c^+ \bar{c} \\
&= J_s f(s) - J_s f(s) - K_c \bar{c} = -K_c \bar{c}
\end{align*}
With $\bar{c}(0) = 0$, the unique solution is $\bar{c}(t) = 0$ for all $t \geq 0$. Since $\mu(t) \geq 0$ is maintained by the projection, we obtain $c(s_t) = \bar{c}(t) - \mu(t) = -\mu(t) \leq 0$.
\end{proof}

\textbf{Remark (Implementation).} In practice, we use the damped pseudoinverse $\hat{J}_c^+ = J_c^\top (J_c J_c^\top + \epsilon I)^{-1}$ with small $\epsilon > 0$ for numerical stability. This introduces a residual $\| J_c \hat{J}_c^+ - I_k \| = O(\epsilon)$, so the exact invariance $\bar{c}(t) = 0$ becomes $\bar{c}(t) = O(\epsilon)$. The correction term $-K_c \hat{J}_c^+ \bar{c}$ actively drives accumulated drift back toward the manifold, maintaining low constraint violation for sufficiently small $\epsilon$.

\textbf{Remark (Discrete time).} Discrete-time application of~\eqref{eq:safe_action} at timestep $\Delta t$ introduces bounded error $O(\Delta t^2)$ per step. Combined with the correction term, constraint satisfaction is maintained for sufficiently small $\Delta t$ relative to $K_c$.

\subsubsection{Reward Calibration}

We calibrate the reward based on correction magnitude:
\begin{equation}
    R_{\text{calibrated}} = R(s_t, a_{\text{safe}}) - \lambda \| a_{\text{unsafe}} - a_{\text{safe}} \|_2^2
    \label{eq:reward_calib}
\end{equation}
where $\lambda > 0$ is the penalty weight, $a_{\text{unsafe}}$ is the original policy output, and $a_{\text{safe}}$ is the projected action. This encourages the policy to produce actions that require minimal correction, reducing reliance on the safety filter over time.

\begin{algorithm}[t]
\caption{Safe RL with Constraint Manifold Projection}
\label{alg:cosmos}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\State \textbf{Input:} Constraint function $c(s)$, gains $K_c$, $\lambda$
\State \textbf{Initialize:} Policy $\pi_\theta$, feasible state $s_0$, slack $\mu_0$
\For{each episode}
    \For{each timestep $t$}
        \State Sample policy action: $a_{\text{unsafe}} \sim \pi_\theta(\cdot | s_t)$
        \State Construct $u_{\text{ref}} = [a_{\text{unsafe}}^\top, \mathbf{0}^\top]^\top$
        \State Compute Jacobians: $J_s$, $J_\mu = I_k$, $J_c$, $N_c$
        \State Project to safe action via~\eqref{eq:safe_action}: $a_{\text{safe}}$
        \State Execute $a_{\text{safe}}$, observe $s_{t+1}$, $r_t$
        \State Compute calibrated reward via~\eqref{eq:reward_calib}
        \State Store transition, update policy
    \EndFor
\EndFor
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\subsection{Safe Reachability Analysis}
\label{sec:reachability}

The constraint manifold filter requires knowledge of obstacle positions, which we assume available as a prior map of static obstacles (e.g., from offline mapping or simulation ground truth). To further construct environment-aware safe regions that adaptively modulate safety margins, we employ offline HJ reachability analysis~\cite{bansal2017hj_reachability}.

\subsubsection{From HJ PDE to Feasibility Value Functions}

In continuous time, given a constraint function $c(s) \leq 0$ defining the safe set $\mathcal{K} = \{s : c(s) \leq 0\}$ and dynamics $\dot{s} = f(s, a)$, the Hamilton-Jacobi variational inequality (HJ-VI) characterizes the \emph{backward reachable tube} (BRT)---the set of initial states from which no control can prevent eventual constraint violation~\cite{bansal2017hj_reachability}:
\begin{equation}
    \max\!\Big\{c(s) - V(s),\; \min_{a} \nabla_s V(s)^\top f(s, a)\Big\} = 0
    \label{eq:hj_vi}
\end{equation}
The zero sublevel set $\{s : V(s) \leq 0\}$ is the maximal \emph{viability kernel}---the largest controlled-invariant subset of $\mathcal{K}$.

For discrete-time systems $s_{t+1} = f(s_t, a_t)$, the HJ-VI~\eqref{eq:hj_vi} reduces to the fixed-point equation $V(s) = \max\{c(s),\, \min_a V(f(s,a))\}$. We define the corresponding optimal feasible value function:
\begin{equation}
    V^*_c(s) := \min_{\pi} \max_{t \in \mathbb{N}} c(s_t), \quad s_0 = s
    \label{eq:value_c}
\end{equation}

A state $s$ is \emph{feasible} if $V^*_c(s) \leq 0$, meaning there exists at least one policy that keeps the system within $\mathcal{K}$ indefinitely. The maximal feasible region (viability kernel) is:
\begin{equation}
    \mathcal{S}^*_f := \{s \in \mathcal{S} \mid V^*_c(s) \leq 0\}
\end{equation}

\subsubsection{Offline Approximation}

We introduce a discount factor $\gamma \in (0, 1)$ to ensure contraction of the Bellman operator. The discounted feasible Bellman operator corresponding to the BRT fixed point is:
\begin{equation}
    \mathcal{T}^* Q_c(s, a) \!:=\! (1{-}\gamma) c(s) + \gamma \max\{c(s), V_c(s')\}
    \label{eq:bellman}
\end{equation}
where $s' = f(s,a)$ is the successor state and $\max\{c(s), \cdot\}$ mirrors the $\max$ in the HJ-VI~\eqref{eq:hj_vi}: the value cannot drop below the current constraint violation, ensuring the BRT structure is preserved.

We use expectile regression~\cite{kostrikov2022iql} to handle out-of-distribution actions in the offline dataset $\mathcal{D}$, approximating the $\min_a$ in the HJ-VI without requiring explicit minimization:
\begin{align}
    L_{V_c} &= \mathbb{E}_{(s,a) \sim \mathcal{D}} \left[ L^\tau_{\text{rev}}(Q_c(s,a) - V_c(s)) \right] \label{eq:loss_v} \\
    L_{Q_c} &= \mathbb{E}_{(s,a,s') \sim \mathcal{D}} \left[ (\mathcal{T}^* Q_c - Q_c(s,a))^2 \right] \label{eq:loss_q}
\end{align}
where $L^\tau_{\text{rev}}(u) = |\tau - \mathbf{1}(u > 0)| \cdot u^2$ with $\tau \in [0.5, 1]$ gives higher weight to smaller $Q_c$ values, biasing toward the safest (most optimistic) actions---the discrete analog of $\min_a$ in the HJ-VI.

\subsubsection{Integration with Safety Filter}

The learned $V^*_c$ modulates the safety filter's conservatism at deployment. We define a smooth weighting function $w(s) = \exp(V^*_c(s) / \delta)$ with scale parameter $\delta > 0$, and scale the danger radius as:
\begin{equation}
    d_{\text{danger}}(s) = d_0 + \eta \, w(s)
    \label{eq:danger_radius}
\end{equation}
where $d_0$ is the base radius and $\eta > 0$ controls the margin. Within the feasible region ($V^*_c \leq 0$), $w \in (0, 1]$: states near the boundary ($V^*_c \approx 0$) have $w \approx 1$ and receive the full enlarged margin $d_0 + \eta$, while states deep in the safe interior ($V^*_c \ll 0$) have $w \approx 0$ and use approximately the nominal margin $d_0$. For infeasible states ($V^*_c > 0$), $w > 1$ further increases conservatism. This enables the filter to be \emph{environment-aware} at deployment without requiring real-time obstacle detection or tracking---the pre-computed $V^*_c$ encodes the reachability structure of the static environment from the prior map.

\subsection{Data-Driven State Estimation}
\label{sec:ekf}

Accurate state estimation is critical for safe navigation. Position sensors (e.g., GPS, visual odometry) suffer from noise that varies with operating conditions---faster motion increases vibration, multipath effects, and motion blur, leading to \emph{velocity-dependent} measurement uncertainty. We model this as:
\begin{equation}
    \sigma(v) = \sigma_{\text{base}}(1 + k \|v\|)
    \label{eq:vel_noise}
\end{equation}
where $\sigma_{\text{base}}$ is the baseline noise standard deviation, $k > 0$ is the velocity scaling coefficient, and $\|v\|$ is the robot speed. A standard EKF with fixed noise covariance $R = \sigma_{\text{base}}^2 I$ cannot adapt to this variation, leading to over-trust of noisy measurements during fast motion. We propose a data-driven EKF that learns to predict the noise covariance from recent IMU data.

\subsubsection{Extended Kalman Filter}

We employ an EKF that linearizes the nonlinear system dynamics around the current state estimate:
\begin{align}
    \hat{s}_{t|t-1} &= f(\hat{s}_{t-1}, a_{t-1}) \\
    P_{t|t-1} &= F_t P_{t-1} F_t^\top + Q_t
\end{align}
where $F_t = \frac{\partial f}{\partial s}\big|_{\hat{s}_{t-1}}$ is the Jacobian of the dynamics and $Q_t$ is the process noise covariance. The EKF state vector extends the navigation state with orientation and angular velocity: $s = [x,y,\theta,v_x,v_y,\omega]^\top \in \mathbb{R}^6$. The measurement vector $y_t = [x_{\text{GPS}}, y_{\text{GPS}}, \theta_{\text{compass}}]^\top \in \mathbb{R}^3$ fuses GPS position with compass heading via the linear observation model $h(s) = H s$ where $H = [I_3 \mid \mathbf{0}_{3\times3}]$. The Kalman update is:
\begin{align}
    K_t &= P_{t|t-1} H^\top (H P_{t|t-1} H^\top + R_t)^{-1} \\
    \hat{s}_t &= \hat{s}_{t|t-1} + K_t(y_t - H\hat{s}_{t|t-1})
\end{align}

\subsubsection{Learned Noise Parameters}

Rather than manually tuning the measurement noise covariance $R_t$, we learn it using a CNN (NoiseAdapter) that processes a sliding window of IMU measurements:
\begin{equation}
    z_t = \text{CNN}\left(\{\omega^{\text{IMU}}_i, a^{\text{IMU}}_i\}_{i=t-L}^{t}\right)
    \label{eq:noise_cnn}
\end{equation}
where $L{=}10$ is the window length (1\,s at 10\,Hz). The NoiseAdapter is a lightweight CNN (${\sim}$5K parameters): two Conv1d layers ($6{\to}32{\to}64$, kernel size 3) with ReLU, followed by adaptive average pooling and two fully connected layers ($64{\to}64{\to}2$), producing $z_t = [z^{\text{lat}}_t, z^{\text{hdg}}_t]^\top \in \mathbb{R}^2$. The adaptive measurement covariance is:
\begin{equation}
    R_{t} = \text{diag}\!\Big(\sigma^2_{\text{lat}}\,\phi(z^{\text{lat}}_t),\;\sigma^2_{\text{lat}}\,\phi(z^{\text{lat}}_t),\;\sigma^2_{\text{hdg}}\,\phi(z^{\text{hdg}}_t)\Big)
    \label{eq:learned_R}
\end{equation}
where $\phi(z) = 10^{\beta \tanh(z)}$, $\sigma_{\text{lat}}, \sigma_{\text{hdg}}$ are baseline noise estimates for position and heading, the first two entries share $z^{\text{lat}}_t$ (isotropic GPS noise), and $\beta > 0$ controls the adaptation range. The $\tanh$ nonlinearity bounds the output, while the exponential parameterization ensures $R_t \succ 0$. When $z_t \approx 0$ (e.g., before convergence), $R_t$ defaults to the baseline covariance, ensuring stability.

\subsubsection{NoiseAdapter Pretraining}

The CNN is pretrained offline on tuples $(\mathbf{w}_t, \tilde{y}_t, y_t)$ from random exploration, where $\mathbf{w}_t \in \mathbb{R}^{L \times 6}$ is the IMU window, $\tilde{y}_t$ is the noisy measurement, and $y_t$ is ground truth. Training minimizes negative log-likelihood:
\begin{equation}
    \mathcal{L}_{\text{NLL}} \!=\! \frac{1}{|\mathcal{D}|} \!\sum_{(\mathbf{w}, \tilde{y}, y)}\! \Big[ \log |R(\mathbf{w})| + (\tilde{y} {-} y)^\top R(\mathbf{w})^{-1} (\tilde{y} {-} y) \Big]
    \label{eq:nll_loss}
\end{equation}
This loss encourages predicting large $R$ during high-speed motion and small $R$ at low speed, directly learning the velocity--noise correlation. Pretraining data is collected from 30 random exploration episodes in Safety-Gymnasium, where sensor noise is injected as additive Gaussian: $\tilde{y}_t = y_t + \epsilon_t$ with $\epsilon_t \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma^2(v_t)I)$ following~\eqref{eq:vel_noise}.

\subsubsection{Baselines}

We compare against established safe RL algorithms in both single-agent and multi-agent settings:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{PPO}~\cite{schulman2017ppo}: Proximal Policy Optimization (unconstrained)
    \item \textbf{PCPO}~\cite{yang2020pcpo}: Projection-based Constrained Policy Optimization, which projects policy updates onto the constraint-satisfying half-space
    \item \textbf{PPO-Lag}~\cite{stooke2020responsive}: PPO with PID Lagrangian constraint penalty
    \item \textbf{TRPO-Lag}~\cite{achiam2017cpo}: Trust Region Policy Optimization with Lagrangian constraints
    \item \textbf{Multi-agent}: MAPPO, HAPPO~\cite{kuba2022happo}, MACPO~\cite{gu2023macpo}, and MAPPO-Lag for the multi-agent Goal task
\end{itemize}
We note that ATACOM~\cite{liu2022atacom} requires task-specific constraint Jacobians and an augmented action space, making direct comparison on the Safety-Gymnasium benchmark non-trivial; our method uses a simplified distance-based filter that approximates the manifold projection for point-robot tasks.

\textbf{Key Observations.}
Our method (PPO\_CM) achieves the \textbf{lowest cost} across all tasks and penalty settings, demonstrating strong \emph{robustness to penalty tuning}. In contrast, baselines exhibit significant cost sensitivity: PPO-Lag's cost on Goal ranges from $5.46$ ($r_{\text{col}}=0.1$) to $13.91$ ($r_{\text{col}}=0.5$), a $2.5\times$ fluctuation. On Circle and Push, baselines exhibit even larger cost ($5$--$38$ range), while our method maintains near-zero cost ($1.38$--$1.55$ on Circle, $0.92$--$1.23$ on Push). The slight cost increase at lower penalties ($r_{\text{col}}{=}0.1$) reflects that the RL policy explores more aggressively when collision penalties are reduced, producing more challenging inputs for the safety filter. PPO\_CM's episode length remains stable (${\sim}350$ steps on Goal) regardless of $r_{\text{col}}$, because the safety filter enforces velocity limits near obstacles independent of the penalty; in contrast, baselines shorten episodes at low penalties by taking riskier, faster paths. Notably, PPO\_CM achieves competitive reward (highest on Goal and Push) while being the only method with consistently near-zero constraint violations.

\textbf{Multi-agent results.}
On Multi-Goal with 2 agents, all baselines incur substantial cost ($12.48$--$36.13$) due to inter-agent and obstacle collisions. PPO\_CM reduces cost to near-zero ($0.07$) while maintaining positive reward, demonstrating that our constraint manifold filter extends naturally to multi-agent settings where collision avoidance must account for both static obstacles and other agents.

\section{CONCLUSIONS}

We presented a safe RL framework that decouples safety enforcement from policy learning through three complementary components: a constraint manifold filter that projects actions into the feasible set via null-space projection, offline HJ reachability analysis that precomputes safe regions to enlarge safety margins, and a data-driven EKF with CNN-learned measurement covariance for robust state estimation under sensor noise. Both the reachability value function and the noise model are pretrained offline, incurring no runtime overhead during RL training. Experiments on Safety-Gymnasium demonstrate near-zero constraint violations across Goal, Circle, and Push tasks under varying penalty settings, with ablation studies confirming 81\% cost reduction on the Goal task when all components are combined. The safety filter and EKF transfer zero-shot to a Webots E-puck robot, achieving zero collisions and 85\% navigation success using only a P-controller, demonstrating policy-agnostic transferability. Current limitations include the restriction to static obstacles and the need for offline pretraining data. Future work includes deployment on physical robots and extension to dynamic obstacles via time-varying reachability analysis.
